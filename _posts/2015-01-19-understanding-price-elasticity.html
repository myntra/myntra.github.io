Discounts, coupons, deals, offers, etc. are the ways retailers have been attracting hordes of consumers ever since. E-tailers, the much recent entrants in the retail business, however, have taken these offerings to the next level owing to many game changing factors at their disposal - leveraging on advantages arising out of fundamentally different ways they conduct the retail business. As the reach of these offerings is quite broad in the online e-tail domain, we need to carefully understand the impact of discounting on the overall sales and revenue.

We at data science team in Myntra are looking at sales patterns to build intelligent and data driven strategies towards pricing. It is known that demand for a good varies with its pricing, “elasticity” helps us understand this variation. Hence, as a first step, modeling the price elasticity of demand for our products would give us better understanding of our catalogue from a pricing perspective and provide a rich framework for pricing projects such as: portfolio optimisation, simulation experiments in pricing, dynamic adaptive pricing, etc.


What is Elasticity?

Elasticity is defined as the ratio of percentage change in the quantity sold to percentage change in price. E.g. If 5% drop in prices for a style brings 10% increase in the number of styles sold, the elasticity for that style would be -2, the negative sign signifying that the direction of change sales is opposite to the direction of change in prices. In this sense, +ve elasticity value for a style means that increasing discount on such styles would lead to a reduction in its sales. Though this needs to be taken with a pinch of salt, we can certainly understand that highly discounting such products may not yield the desired lift in their sales.

For example consider a few categories shown in the below figure, where the size of the bubble represents their absolute elasticity values. Categories on the left, where models yielded positive elasticity, have peculiar characteristics: they cater to users’ aspirational needs (e.g. ring); would have sold anyways (e.g. dupatta, ties) or these seem to be products where users don’t want to go wrong in their purchase and hence don’t rely on discount based purchase (e.g. inner wear). However note that, at a broad category level, as discussed above, these insights can provide only broad guidelines, and for more actionable insights we need to build models at a much granular level.
  
Granular modeling of elasticity for actionable insights

For actionable insights from elasticity models we would like to know how different styles within a category behave at different pricing levels. For this we need to build elasticity models for specific groups of styles. Determining this group of products for which we can assume same elasticity, and study them together, is tricky. A particular style can’t be siloed, and studied for its elasticity, completely independent of other styles, which are very similar to it. We also run into risk of data sparsity at a very fine grain level view for studying elasticity of products and may miss out on a possibly emerging pattern had we taken a coarser look. To do this wisely we leverage on how users perceived the set of styles to which they were exposed to, and use their crowd curated ‘collection’ of styles perceived as similar on multitude of attributes such as brand, article, styling, colour, etc. These collections could be roughly imagined as aisles in a store. Sometimes looking at these aisles suffices to study price elasticity and sometimes we need to resort to the ‘racks’ within these aisles and study elasticity for group of neighborhood styles placed on those racks when the aisle as a whole can’t provide a coherent picture w.r.t elasticity. In the below figure, we can see how granular models for Kook N Keech Disney have different behaviors w.r.t elasticity. It is typically observed that higher price band KnK Disney styles have positive elasticity. This fact, coupled with the observation in http://sartorialscience.myntrablogs.com/2014/11/understanding-brand-positioning.html, that KnK Disney is a low entropy brand, could indicate that there may be a interest group for KnK Disney who are not just driven by discounts but are interested in the unique offering such styles have to provide and don’t care only about discounts while making their purchase decision. On the other hand, collections of brand Eavan, stay consistent in their elasticity value when modeled at more granular sub-collection level.

How are these models built?

There are quite a few challenges in modeling elasticity, as there are many factors which together control the sales happening at a point of time. We need to control for these factors while studying elasticity. Some of these factors are:
•  Momentum: a trend in sales is observed for certain article types which needs to be factored e.g. Jackets show a trend during winters
•   Day of Week Factor: for certain articles day of week seems to play a role
•   Traffic Volatility: Marketing and other visibility factors govern the amount of traffic that a style begets, which in-turn affects its sales; we need to control for such factors while studying price elasticity.

There are many applications and also many pertinent observations that arise out of such elasticity modeling; we would discuss these in the forth-coming articles.
